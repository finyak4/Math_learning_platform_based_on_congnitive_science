1. Antiderivatives and Indefinite Integrals

An antiderivative of a function \( f(x) \) is a function \( F(x) \) such that \( F'(x) = f(x) \). The collection of all antiderivatives of \( f \) is called the indefinite integral.

Indefinite Integral
$$ \int f(x) \, dx = F(x) + C $$

where \( C \) is the constant of integration.

Basic Integration Rules
\( \int x^n \, dx = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + C \quad (n \neq -1) \)
\( \int \frac{1}{x} \, dx = \ln|x| + C \)
\( \int e^x \, dx = e^x + C \)
\( \int \sin x \, dx = -\cos x + C \)
\( \int \cos x \, dx = \sin x + C \)

Example: Power Rule

Evaluate \( \int (3x^2 + 5) \, dx \).

Solution:

$$ \int (3x^2 + 5) \, dx = 3 \cdot \frac{x^3}{3} + 5x + C = x^3 + 5x + C $$

2. The Definite Integral

The definite integral represents the signed area under the curve \( y = f(x) \) from \( x=a \) to \( x=b \). It is defined as the limit of a Riemann sum as the number of partitions approaches infinity.

Definition
$$ \int_{a}^{b} f(x) \, dx = \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{i=1}^{n} f(x_i^*) \Delta x $$
💡
Key Insight: The definite integral gives net signed area—areas above the x-axis are positive, while areas below are negative.

3. The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (FTC)

The FTC establishes the inverse relationship between differentiation and integration, connecting the two fundamental operations of calculus.

FTC Part 1: Derivatives of Integrals
$$ \frac{d}{dx} \int_{a}^{x} f(t) \, dt = f(x) $$

This says: if you integrate a function and then differentiate, you get the original function back.

Example: FTC Part 1

Find the derivative of \( g(x) = \int_{1}^{x} \sec t \, dt \).

Solution: By FTC Part 1:

$$ g'(x) = \sec x $$
FTC Part 2: Evaluation of Definite Integrals
$$ \int_{a}^{b} f(x) \, dx = F(b) - F(a) $$

where \( F \) is any antiderivative of \( f \).

Example: Evaluating a Definite Integral

Evaluate \( \int_{1}^{3} (2x + 1) \, dx \).

Solution:

1. Find the antiderivative: \( F(x) = x^2 + x \)

2. Apply FTC Part 2:

$$ [x^2 + x]_{1}^{3} = (3^2 + 3) - (1^2 + 1) = 12 - 2 = 10 $$

4. The Net Change Theorem

The integral of a rate of change gives the net change in the quantity. This is a direct application of FTC Part 2.

Net Change
$$ \int_{a}^{b} F'(x) \, dx = F(b) - F(a) $$

Example: Displacement vs Distance

If \( v(t) = t^2 - 2t \) is the velocity of a particle:

1. Displacement (Net Change):

$$ \int_{0}^{3} v(t) \, dt = \int_{0}^{3} (t^2 - 2t) \, dt = \left[\frac{t^3}{3} - t^2\right]_0^3 = 0 $$

2. Total Distance: We integrate the absolute value of velocity.

Since \( v(t) < 0 \) on \((0, 2)\) and \( v(t) > 0 \) on \((2, 3)\), we split:

$$ \int_{0}^{2} -(t^2 - 2t) \, dt + \int_{2}^{3} (t^2 - 2t) \, dt = \frac{4}{3} + \frac{4}{3} = \frac{8}{3} $$
⚠️
Common Mistake: Don't confuse displacement (net change) with total distance traveled. Displacement can be zero even if the object moved!
