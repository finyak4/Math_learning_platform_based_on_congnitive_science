1. Integration by Substitution (U-Substitution)

The reverse chain rule. Use this when you see a function and its derivative in the integrand.

$$ \int f(g(x)) g'(x) \, dx = \int f(u) \, du $$

where \( u = g(x) \) and \( du = g'(x) \, dx \).

Example: Definite Integral with U-Sub

Evaluate \( \int_{0}^{4} \sqrt{2x+1} \, dx \).

Solution: Let \( u = 2x+1 \), then \( du = 2dx \) or \( dx = du/2 \).

Change limits: \( x=0 \to u=1 \), \( x=4 \to u=9 \).

$$ \int_{1}^{9} \sqrt{u} \cdot \frac{du}{2} = \frac{1}{2} \left[\frac{2}{3} u^{3/2}\right]_1^9 = \frac{1}{3} (27 - 1) = \frac{26}{3} $$

2. Integration by Parts

The reverse product rule. Useful for products of different types of functions (e.g., polynomial times exponential).

Integration by Parts Formula
$$ \int u \, dv = uv - \int v \, du $$
💡
LIATE Rule for choosing \( u \): Logarithmic, Inverse Trig, Algebraic, Trigonometric, Exponential.

Example: Integration by Parts

Evaluate \( \int x e^x \, dx \).

Solution: Let \( u = x \), \( dv = e^x dx \). Then \( du = dx \), \( v = e^x \).

$$ \int x e^x \, dx = x e^x - \int e^x \, dx = x e^x - e^x + C $$

3. Trigonometric Integrals

Strategies for integrating combinations of powers of sine, cosine, tangent, and secant.

Key Strategies
  • Use identity \( \sin^2 x + \cos^2 x = 1 \) for odd powers of sin/cos.
  • Use half-angle formulas for even powers:
\( \sin^2 x = \frac{1 - \cos 2x}{2} \), \quad \( \cos^2 x = \frac{1 + \cos 2x}{2} \)

Example: Power of Sine

Evaluate \( \int \sin^3 x \, dx \).

Solution: Write \( \sin^3 x = \sin^2 x \cdot \sin x = (1 - \cos^2 x) \sin x \).

Let \( u = \cos x \), \( du = -\sin x dx \).

$$ \int (1 - u^2) (-du) = \int (u^2 - 1) \, du = \frac{1}{3} \cos^3 x - \cos x + C $$

4. Trigonometric Substitution

Use when the integrand contains \( \sqrt{a^2 - x^2} \), \( \sqrt{a^2 + x^2} \), or \( \sqrt{x^2 - a^2} \).

Expression Substitution Identity
\( \sqrt{a^2 - x^2} \) \( x = a \sin \theta \) \( 1 - \sin^2 \theta = \cos^2 \theta \)
\( \sqrt{a^2 + x^2} \) \( x = a \tan \theta \) \( 1 + \tan^2 \theta = \sec^2 \theta \)
\( \sqrt{x^2 - a^2} \) \( x = a \sec \theta \) \( \sec^2 \theta - 1 = \tan^2 \theta \)

Example: Trig Substitution

Evaluate \( \int \frac{\sqrt{9-x^2}}{x^2} \, dx \).

Solution: Let \( x = 3 \sin \theta \), \( dx = 3 \cos \theta \, d\theta \), \( \sqrt{9-x^2} = 3 \cos \theta \).

$$ \int \frac{3 \cos \theta}{9 \sin^2 \theta} (3 \cos \theta) \, d\theta = \int \cot^2 \theta \, d\theta $$

Simplify using \( \cot^2 \theta = \csc^2 \theta - 1 \) and integrate.

5. Partial Fraction Decomposition

Used for integrating rational functions \( \frac{P(x)}{Q(x)} \).

Basic Form

Break down the fraction into simpler parts based on factoring the denominator.

Example: Distinct Linear Factors

Evaluate \( \int \frac{1}{x^2 - 4} \, dx \).

Solution: \( \frac{1}{(x-2)(x+2)} = \frac{A}{x-2} + \frac{B}{x+2} \).

Solving gives \( A = 1/4, B = -1/4 \).

$$ \frac{1}{4} \int \frac{1}{x-2} \, dx - \frac{1}{4} \int \frac{1}{x+2} \, dx = \frac{1}{4} \ln \left| \frac{x-2}{x+2} \right| + C $$

6. Approximate Integration

Techniques to estimate definite integrals when exact antiderivatives cannot be found.

Trapezoidal Rule
$$ T_n = \frac{\Delta x}{2} [f(x_0) + 2f(x_1) + 2f(x_2) + \dots + 2f(x_{n-1}) + f(x_n)] $$
Simpson's Rule (n must be even)
$$ S_n = \frac{\Delta x}{3} [f(x_0) + 4f(x_1) + 2f(x_2) + 4f(x_3) + \dots + 4f(x_{n-1}) + f(x_n)] $$

Example: Trapezoidal Rule

Approximate \( \int_0^4 x^2 \, dx \) using \( T_4 \) (n=4).

Solution: \( \Delta x = \frac{4-0}{4} = 1 \). Points: \( 0, 1, 2, 3, 4 \).

$$ T_4 = \frac{1}{2} [0^2 + 2(1^2) + 2(2^2) + 2(3^2) + 4^2] = \frac{1}{2} \cdot 44 = 22 $$

(Exact value is \( 64/3 \approx 21.33 \))

7. Improper Integrals

Integrals with infinite intervals or discontinuous integrands.

Type 1: Infinite Interval
$$ \int_{a}^{\infty} f(x) \, dx = \lim_{t \to \infty} \int_{a}^{t} f(x) \, dx $$

Example: Convergent Improper Integral

Evaluate \( \int_{1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{x^2} \, dx \).

Solution:

$$ \lim_{t \to \infty} \left[-\frac{1}{x}\right]_1^t = \lim_{t \to \infty} \left(-\frac{1}{t} + 1\right) = 1 $$

The integral converges to 1.

Type 2: Discontinuous Integrand

If \( f \) is discontinuous at \( b \):

$$ \int_{a}^{b} f(x) \, dx = \lim_{t \to b^-} \int_{a}^{t} f(x) \, dx $$
