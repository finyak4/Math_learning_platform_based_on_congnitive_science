1. The Definition of a Derivative

The derivative is the fundamental tool of calculus used to measure how a function changes at any specific instant. While algebra calculates the average rate of change (slope of a secant line between two points), calculus calculates the instantaneous rate of change (slope of the tangent line at a single point).

Mathematically, this is found by taking the limit as the distance (\(h\)) between two points on the curve shrinks to zero.

The Limit Definition
$$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}$$
💡
Notation Reference:
  • Lagrange (\(f'(x), y'\)): emphasizes the derivative as a function.
  • Leibniz (\(\frac{dy}{dx}\)): emphasizes the relationship "change in y divided by change in x".

2. Basic Power & Polynomial Rules

Most algebraic functions rely on the Power Rule. This pattern allows you to differentiate polynomials without using the long limit definition. It works for any real number exponent \(n\), including fractions (roots) and negatives (reciprocals).

Power Rule: \(\frac{d}{dx}(x^n) = n \cdot x^{n-1}\)
Constant Rule: \(\frac{d}{dx}(c) = 0\)

Examples

1. Simple Polynomial: \(f(x) = x^4\)

$$f'(x) = 4x^3$$

2. Radical (Rewrite first): \(y = \sqrt{x} = x^{1/2}\)

$$y' = \frac{1}{2}x^{-1/2} = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}$$

3. Rational (Rewrite first): \(y = \frac{1}{x^3} = x^{-3}\)

$$y' = -3x^{-4} = -\frac{3}{x^4}$$

3. Exponential & Logarithmic Rules

Exponential functions describe growth proportional to current size. The function \(e^x\) is unique because it is its own derivative—its rate of growth is exactly equal to its value. Logarithmic functions are the inverses of exponentials.

\(\frac{d}{dx}(e^x) = e^x\)   |   \(\frac{d}{dx}(\ln x) = \frac{1}{x}\)

Examples

1. Mixed Terms: \(y = 3e^x - 2\ln x\)

$$y' = 3e^x - \frac{2}{x}$$

2. With Coefficients: \(y = \frac{1}{2}\ln x\)

$$y' = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{x} = \frac{1}{2x}$$

4. Trigonometric Derivatives

Trigonometric derivatives model cyclic or oscillating motion (like pendulums or waves). Memorizing these requires recognizing patterns: sine and cosine pair together, tangent and secant pair together, and all "co-functions" result in a negative derivative.

\(\frac{d}{dx}(\sin x) = \cos x\)   |   \(\frac{d}{dx}(\cos x) = -\sin x\)
\(\frac{d}{dx}(\tan x) = \sec^2 x\)   |   \(\frac{d}{dx}(\cot x) = -\csc^2 x\)
\(\frac{d}{dx}(\sec x) = \sec x \tan x\)   |   \(\frac{d}{dx}(\csc x) = -\csc x \cot x\)

Examples

1. Basic Sum: \(y = \sin x + \tan x\)

$$y' = \cos x + \sec^2 x$$

2. Negative Check: \(y = 4\cos x\)

$$y' = 4(-\sin x) = -4\sin x$$

5. Product & Quotient Rules

When functions are multiplied or divided, you cannot simply take the derivative of each part. The product rule accounts for how the two changing quantities interact, adding their contributions together. The quotient rule is similar but involves subtraction due to the division.

Product Rule \((f \cdot g)\)
$$(fg)' = f'g + fg'$$
Quotient Rule \((\frac{f}{g})\)
$$\left(\frac{f}{g}\right)' = \frac{g f' - f g'}{g^2}$$

Examples

1. Product: \(y = x^2 \sin x\)

$$y' = (2x)(\sin x) + (x^2)(\cos x)$$

2. Quotient: \(y = \frac{x^2}{3x+1}\)

$$y' = \frac{(3x+1)(2x) - (x^2)(3)}{(3x+1)^2} = \frac{3x^2+2x}{(3x+1)^2}$$

6. The Chain Rule

The Chain Rule is used for composite functions, where one function is nested inside another \(f(g(x))\). It works by multiplying the rate of change of the outer function by the rate of change of the inner function.

$$\frac{d}{dx}[f(g(x))] = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)$$

Logic: Derivative of Outside \(\times\) Derivative of Inside.

Examples

1. Power Chain: \(y = (3x^2 + 1)^7\)

  • Outer: \((\dots)^7 \rightarrow 7(\dots)^6\)
  • Inner: \(3x^2 + 1 \rightarrow 6x\)
$$y' = 7(3x^2 + 1)^6 \cdot (6x) = 42x(3x^2 + 1)^6$$

2. Trig Chain: \(y = \cos(x^3)\)

  • Outer: \(\cos(\dots) \rightarrow -\sin(\dots)\)
  • Inner: \(x^3 \rightarrow 3x^2\)
$$y' = -\sin(x^3) \cdot (3x^2) = -3x^2 \sin(x^3)$$

7. Higher Order Derivatives

Differentiation is an operation that can be repeated. If you derive the first derivative, you get the second derivative. This is critical in physics for connecting position, velocity, and acceleration.

  • First Derivative \((f')\): Rate of Change (Velocity).
  • Second Derivative \((f'')\): Rate of Change of the Velocity (Acceleration).

Example: Motion

Given Position \(s(t) = t^3 - 3t^2\)

1. Velocity (First Derivative):

$$v(t) = s'(t) = 3t^2 - 6t$$

2. Acceleration (Second Derivative):

$$a(t) = v'(t) = 6t - 6$$
