Definition of a Derivative

The derivative measures the instantaneous rate of change of a function. Geometrically, it is the slope of the tangent line to the curve at a specific point \((x)\).

Formal Definition (Limit Definition)
$$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}$$
💡
Notation: Lagrange: \(f'(x)\) or \(y'\) | Leibniz: \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) or \(\frac{d}{dx}[f(x)]\)

The Power Rule

A shortcut for differentiating polynomials \(x^n\), avoiding the long limit process.

$$\frac{d}{dx}(x^n) = n \cdot x^{n-1}$$

Steps: 1. Bring exponent down. 2. Subtract 1 from exponent.

Examples

1. Basic Polynomial: \(f(x) = x^4\)

$$f'(x) = 4x^{4-1} = \mathbf{4x^3}$$

2. With Coefficient: \(y = 5x^3\)

$$y' = 5 \cdot (3x^2) = \mathbf{15x^2}$$
⚠️
Rewriting Required: Always rewrite roots and fractions as powers first.
\(\frac{1}{x^3} \rightarrow x^{-3}\)  |  \(\sqrt{x} \rightarrow x^{1/2}\)

Sum & Difference Rule

If you have multiple terms, take the derivative of each one separately.

Example

$$f(x) = 2x^5 - 4x^3 + 7x - 10$$
$$f'(x) = 10x^4 - 12x^2 + 7 - 0$$

Note: Derivative of constant is 0. Derivative of \(x\) is 1.

Product Rule

Used when two functions are multiplied: \(f(x) \cdot g(x)\).

$$(f \cdot g)' = f'g + fg'$$

"Derivative of first · second + first · Derivative of second"

Example: \(y = (x^3)(2x + 5)\)

  • \(f = x^3 \rightarrow f' = 3x^2\)
  • \(g = 2x + 5 \rightarrow g' = 2\)
$$y' = (3x^2)(2x+5) + (x^3)(2)$$
$$y' = \mathbf{8x^3 + 15x^2}$$

Quotient Rule

Used when two functions are divided: \(\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}\).

$$\left(\frac{f}{g}\right)' = \frac{f'g - fg'}{g^2}$$

"Lo d-Hi minus Hi d-Lo, over Lo Lo"

Example: \(y = \frac{5x^2}{3x + 1}\)

$$y' = \frac{(3x+1)(10x) - (5x^2)(3)}{(3x+1)^2} = \mathbf{\frac{15x^2 + 10x}{(3x+1)^2}}$$

Chain Rule

Used for composite functions: \(f(g(x))\).

$$\frac{d}{dx}[f(g(x))] = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)$$

"Derivative of OUTSIDE (keep inside same) \(\times\) Derivative of INSIDE"

Example: \(y = (3x^2 + 1)^7\)

  • Outside: \((\dots)^7 \rightarrow 7(\dots)^6\)
  • Inside: \(3x^2 + 1 \rightarrow 6x\)
$$y' = 7(3x^2 + 1)^6 \cdot (6x) = \mathbf{42x(3x^2 + 1)^6}$$

Trigonometric Derivatives

The Big Two:

\(\frac{d}{dx}(\sin x) = \cos x\)   |   \(\frac{d}{dx}(\cos x) = -\sin x\)

The Others:

  • \(\tan x \rightarrow \sec^2 x\)
  • \(\cot x \rightarrow -\csc^2 x\)
  • \(\sec x \rightarrow \sec x \tan x\)
  • \(\csc x \rightarrow -\csc x \cot x\)

Tip: "Co" functions always return a negative.

Mixed Example (Product + Trig)

\(y = x^2 \sin x\)

$$y' = (2x)(\sin x) + (x^2)(\cos x)$$
