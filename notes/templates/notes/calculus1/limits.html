1. Intuitive Definition of a Limit

A limit asks: "What value is the function approaching as \(x\) gets closer and closer to a specific number?" Crucially, limits care about the journey (the approach), not the destination (the actual value at that point). The function does not need to be defined at the specific point for the limit to exist.

Notation
$$\lim_{x \to 3} f(x) = 5$$

Read as: "The limit of \(f(x)\) as \(x\) approaches 3 is 5." (Even if \(f(3)\) is undefined).

🚦
The Two-Sided Requirement: For the general limit to exist, the Left approach must equal the Right approach.
\(\lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) = \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x)\)
If they point to different numbers (like a jump in the graph), the Limit Does Not Exist (DNE).

2. Basic Limit Laws

Limit laws allow you to break complex functions into simpler pieces. You don't need to calculate the whole function at once; you can find the limit of each part separately and then combine them.

Algebraic Rules
Sum Rule: $$\lim_{x \to c} [f(x) + g(x)] = \lim_{x \to c} f(x) + \lim_{x \to c} g(x)$$
Difference Rule: $$\lim_{x \to c} [f(x) - g(x)] = \lim_{x \to c} f(x) - \lim_{x \to c} g(x)$$
Product Rule: $$\lim_{x \to c} [f(x) \cdot g(x)] = \left[\lim_{x \to c} f(x)\right] \cdot \left[\lim_{x \to c} g(x)\right]$$
Quotient Rule: $$\lim_{x \to c} \left[\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}\right] = \frac{\lim_{x \to c} f(x)}{\lim_{x \to c} g(x)} \quad (\text{if denominator} \neq 0)$$

Examples of Applying Laws

1. Breaking down a Polynomial:

$$\lim_{x \to 2} (3x^2 + 5x)$$

Using Sum and Constant laws:

$$3 \cdot [\lim_{x \to 2} x^2] + 5 \cdot [\lim_{x \to 2} x] = 3(4) + 5(2) = 12 + 10 = \mathbf{22}$$

2. Product Law:

$$\lim_{x \to \pi} (x \cos x) = (\lim_{x \to \pi} x) \cdot (\lim_{x \to \pi} \cos x) = \pi \cdot (-1) = \mathbf{-\pi}$$

3. Strategy 1: Direct Substitution

The first step for any limit problem is to simply plug the number in. This relies on the concept of Continuity. If a function has no breaks or holes at the specific point, the Limit is equal to the Function Value.

The Substitution Rule
\(\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a)\)

(Valid only if the function is continuous at \(a\))

Examples

1. Polynomials (Always Continuous):

$$\lim_{x \to 3} (2x^2 - 4) = 2(3)^2 - 4 = 18 - 4 = 14$$

2. Transcendental (Continuous on domain):

$$\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\cos x}{e^x} = \frac{\cos(0)}{e^0} = \frac{1}{1} = 1$$
⚠️
When does this fail? If plugging in the number gives you \(\frac{0}{0}\) (Indeterminate) or \(\frac{5}{0}\) (Undefined/Infinity), substitution fails. You must use Strategy 2.

4. Strategy 2: Indeterminate Forms (0/0)

If direct substitution results in \(\frac{0}{0}\), it implies there is a Removable Discontinuity (a hole) in the graph. The limit exists, but algebra is required to "fill the hole" (cancel the problematic term) before substituting.

[Image of removable discontinuity graph vs infinite discontinuity]
Technique A: Factoring

Used for Rational Functions (Polynomial fractions). Factor top and bottom to cancel the \((x-a)\) term.

Example: Factoring

$$\lim_{x \to 2} \frac{x^2 - 4}{x - 2}$$

1. Try Substitution: \(\frac{2^2 - 4}{2 - 2} = \frac{0}{0}\) (Indeterminate).

2. Factor and Cancel:

$$\lim_{x \to 2} \frac{(x-2)(x+2)}{(x-2)} = \lim_{x \to 2} (x+2)$$

3. Retry Substitution: \(2 + 2 = \mathbf{4}\)

Technique B: Conjugates

Used when Square Roots are involved. Multiply numerator and denominator by the conjugate \(\sqrt{A} + B\).

Example: Conjugates

$$\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sqrt{x+9} - 3}{x}$$

Multiplying by \(\frac{\sqrt{x+9} + 3}{\sqrt{x+9} + 3}\) removes the root from the top, allowing you to cancel the \(x\) on the bottom.

5. Limits at Infinity (End Behavior)

When \(x \to \infty\), we aren't looking for a specific point, but for the Horizontal Asymptote. For fractions, this turns into a battle of dominance between the numerator (top) and denominator (bottom).

The Degree Test
  • Bottom Degree > Top: \(\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x^2}{x^5} = 0\) (Bottom wins)
  • Degrees Equal: \(\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{3x^2}{2x^2} = \frac{3}{2}\) (Tie: Ratio of coefficients)
  • Top Degree > Bottom: \(\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x^5}{x^2} = \infty\) (Top wins, no limit)

6. Special Trigonometric Limits

These are specific cases where substitution gives \(\frac{0}{0}\), but standard factoring doesn't work. These are proven using the Squeeze Theorem and should be recognized on sight.

$$\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin x}{x} = 1$$
$$\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{1 - \cos x}{x} = 0$$

Example: Adjusting the Argument

$$\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin(7x)}{x}$$

Multiply by \(\frac{7}{7}\) to match the angle:

$$7 \cdot \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin(7x)}{7x} = 7 \cdot 1 = \mathbf{7}$$

7. Continuity

A function is continuous if you can draw its graph without lifting your pen. Calculus defines this strictly: the road must lead to the bridge, and the bridge must be there.

The Continuity Checklist (3 Steps)

A function \(f(x)\) is continuous at \(x=c\) if:

  1. \(f(c)\) is defined. (The point exists)
  2. \(\lim_{x \to c} f(x)\) exists. (The approaches match)
  3. \(\lim_{x \to c} f(x) = f(c)\). (The limit equals the point)

Types of Discontinuities

If the checklist fails, the function is Discontinuous. Here is how to diagnose which type:

Type Math Characteristic Real Example
Removable (Hole) Limit exists, but \(f(c)\) is missing. \(\frac{x^2-9}{x-3}\) at \(x=3\)
Jump Left Limit \(\neq\) Right Limit. Piecewise functions (Steps)
Infinite Limit explodes to \(\infty\). \(\frac{1}{x}\) at \(x=0\)
